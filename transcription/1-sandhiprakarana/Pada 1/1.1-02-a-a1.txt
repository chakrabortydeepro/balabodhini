                    <l style="sūtra" xml:id="KS-1.1.1">
                        <label>KS 1.1.1</label><hi>siddho varṇasamāmnāyaḥ ||1.1.1||</hi>
                    </l>

                    <ab corresp="#KS-1.1.1" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#KS-1.1.1-t">The traditional inventory of speech sounds
                                    (<foreign>varṇa</foreign>) is well-established. </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.1"><label>BB 1.1.1</label><anchor n="BB-1.1.1-t"/>asti
                        siddhakramo varṇānām akārādīnāṃ samāmnāyaḥ pāṭho mātṛkārūpaḥ | asti ca
                        pāṇininā sve tantre pratyāhārārtham aparathā sādhitakramaḥ | tatrāniyame
                        prāpte niyamaẖ kriyate | iha tantre siddhakrama eva varṇapāṭho
                        vakṣyamāṇasvarādisaṃjñānām āśrayatvena grāhyo, na sādhitakrama iti seyam
                        aniyame niyamaḥ paribhāṣā | akṣarāṇāṃ varṇasaṃjñāpy ata eva
                        varṇaśabdoccāraṇād boddhavyā | a ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ ḷ ḹ e ai o au svarāḥ | ka kha
                        ga gha ṅa ca cha ja jha ña ṭa ṭha ḍa ḍha ṇa ta tha da dha na pa pha ba bha
                        ma ya ra la va śa ṣa sa ha aṃ aḥ ẖka ḫpa vyañjanāni | prayojanaṃ sarvatra
                        ||</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.1" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.1-t">The traditional inventory or reading of the
                                speech sounds starting from <foreign>a</foreign> has a
                                well-established order in the form of the regular alphabetical order
                                    (<foreign>Mātṛkā</foreign>). There is also a different one,
                                whose order was constructed by Pāṇini in his own system for the sake
                                of abbreviation (<foreign>pratyāhāra</foreign>). Since under those
                                circumstances, such a lack of regulation has occurred, a restriction
                                is being made. Here in the system (<foreign>Kātantra</foreign>),
                                only the reading of the sounds in the well-established order
                                    (<foreign>siddhakrama</foreign>) and not the one in the
                                constructed order (<foreign>sādhitakrama</foreign>) must be
                                considered as the base for the terms such as
                                    <foreign>svara</foreign>, etc., which are about to be mentioned;
                                hence this is a metarule that is a regulation for the lack of
                                regulation. The letters (<foreign>akṣara</foreign>) have the
                                designation <foreign>varṇa</foreign>, which is understandable by the
                                very utterance of the word <foreign>varṇa</foreign> (in the sūtra).
                                The vowels (<foreign>svara</foreign>) are: <foreign>a ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ
                                    ḷ ḹ e ai o au</foreign>. The consonants
                                    (<foreign>vyañjana</foreign>) are: <foreign>k kh g gh ṅ c ch j
                                    jh ñ ṭ ṭh ḍ ḍh ṇ t th d dh n p ph b bh m y r l v ś ṣ s h ṃ ḥ ẖ
                                    ḫ</foreign>. The application (of the sūtra) is everywhere.
                            </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <l style="sūtra" xml:id="KS-1.1.2">
                        <label>KS 1.1.2</label><anchor n="KS-1.1.2-t"/><hi>tatra caturdaśādau svarāḥ
                            ||1.1.2||</hi></l>


                    <ab corresp="#KS-1.1.2" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#KS-1.1.2-t">In that (sound inventory), the fourteen
                                [speech sounds] in the beginning are vowels
                                    (<foreign>svara</foreign>).</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.2-1"><label>BB 1.1.2</label><anchor n="BB-1.1.2-1-t"/> tatra
                        mātṛkārūpe varṇapāṭhe, ādau ye caturdaśa varṇāḥ, te svarasaṃjñā bhavanti | a
                        ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ ḷ ḹ e ai o au | prayojanaṃ, <hi>ro re lopaṃ svaraś ca pūrvo
                            dīrgha </hi><metamark function="source">[Kā.Sū. 1.5.17]</metamark>
                        ityādi | vī rauti || </p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.2-1" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.2-1-t">In the reading of the speech sounds, that
                                is in the regular alphabetical order, the fourteen sounds in the
                                beginning are called vowels— <foreign>a ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ ḷ ḹ e ai o
                                    au</foreign>. The application is in rules like <foreign><hi>ro
                                        re lopaṃ svaraś ca pūrvo dīrghaḥ</hi> (1.5.17)</foreign>,
                                    “<foreign>r</foreign> followed by <foreign>r</foreign> undergoes
                                deletion and the preceding vowel is lengthened”. (E.g.) <foreign>vī
                                    rauti</foreign> (&#60; <foreign>vir + rauti</foreign>, “a bird
                                calls”).</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.2-2"><anchor n="BB-1.1.2-2-t"/>dātṝjjvāśayatoṣita iti
                        vaktavye, dātḹjjvāśayatoṣita iti bālena śaktivaikalyād yad apabhāṣitam, tad
                        anyo yadānukaroti, bālo dātḹjjvāśayatoṣita ity āheti, tadānukaraṇe nāsti
                        doṣa iti smaraṇāt sādhu bhavati | tatsthasya ḹkārasya svarasaṃjñārthaḥ
                        ḹkārasya pāṭhaḥ | tenātra svarāt parasya vyañjanasya vyañjane pare dvirbhāvo
                        vaktavya iti ḹkārāt parasya jakārasya dvirbhāvaḥ | evaṃ mātḹr ity abravīc
                        chiśur ityādau svaratve sati ḹkārasya nāmitvān <hi>nāmiparo ram</hi>
                        <metamark function="source">[Kā.Sū. 1.5.12]</metamark> iti ḹkārāt parasya
                        visarjanīyasya rephaḥ | </p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.2-2" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.2-2-t">When
                                    <foreign>dātṝjjvāśayatoṣitaḥ</foreign>, “gratified by the
                                donor’s sincere intention,” is to be said, a child mispronounces it
                                as <foreign>dātḹjjvāśayatoṣitaḥ</foreign> due to his incapability.
                                When another person imitates it, by saying, “The boy says,
                                    <foreign>dātḹjjvāśayatoṣitaḥ</foreign>” then it is all right
                                because traditionally there is nothing wrong with imitation. The
                                reading of the sound <foreign>ḹ</foreign> stands for the designation
                                of the sound <foreign>ḹ</foreign> occurring there (in the word
                                    <foreign>dātḹjjvāśayatoṣitaḥ</foreign>) as a vowel. That is why,
                                here, according to the rule that prescribes the doubling of a
                                consonant preceded by a vowel and followed by a consonant, there is
                                doubling of the sound <foreign>j</foreign> preceded by
                                    <foreign>ḹ</foreign>. Thus, in (a sentence) like, <foreign>mātḹr
                                    iti abravīc chiśuḥ</foreign>, “the child said,
                                    <foreign>mātḹḥ</foreign>,” <foreign>ḥ</foreign>
                                    (<foreign>visarjanīya</foreign>) preceded by
                                    <foreign>ḹ</foreign> turns into <foreign>r</foreign> by the rule
                                        <foreign><hi>nāmiparo ram</hi> (1.5.12)</foreign>,
                                    “<foreign>ḥ</foreign> preceded by a <foreign>nāmin</foreign>
                                turns into <foreign>r</foreign>,” because <foreign>ḹ</foreign> is a
                                    <foreign>nāmin</foreign>, since it is a vowel. </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.2-3"><anchor n="BB-1.1.2-3-t"/>tatraśabda uttaratra
                        saṃjñāsamāveśārthaḥ svarasamānasaṃjñe krameṇa mā bhūtām iti |
                        savarṇādisaṃjñānāṃ tu saṃjñino nirdekṣyanta eva ||</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.2-3" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.2-3-t">The word <foreign>tatra</foreign>, “there”
                                (in this <foreign>sūtra</foreign>) is for the sake of a
                                co-designation in the following one so that the designations vowel
                                    (<foreign>svara</foreign>) and monophthong
                                    (<foreign>samāna</foreign>) must not apply in a sequence. The
                                designata (referents) that are designated as homogenous sound
                                    <foreign>savarṇa</foreign> etc., however, will be directly
                                referred to. </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <l style="sūtra" xml:id="KS-1.1.3">
                        <label>KS 1.1.3</label><anchor n="KS-1.1.3-t"/><hi>daśa samānāḥ
                            ||1.1.3||</hi>
                    </l>

                    <l corresp="#KS-1.1.3"><label>anuvṛtti</label><ref target="#KS-1.1.2"
                            >tatra</ref>
                        <ref target="#KS-1.1.2">ādau</ref></l>

                    <ab corresp="#KS-1.1.3" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#KS-1.1.3-t">[In that sound inventory,] the ten [in the
                                beginning] are the monophthongs (<foreign>samāna</foreign>).</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.3"><label>BB 1.1.3</label><anchor n="BB-1.1.3-t"/>tatrādāv ity
                        eva | tatra mātṛkārūpe varṇapāṭhe, ādau ye daśa varṇās te samānasaṃjñā
                        bhavanti | a ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ ḷ ḹ | prayojanam, <hi>samānas savarṇe
                            dīrghībhavati paraś ca lopam </hi><metamark function="source">[Kā.Sū.
                            1.2.1]</metamark> ityādi | śivāptiḥ ||</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.3" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.3-t">Only (the words) <foreign>tatra</foreign>
                                and <foreign>ādau</foreign> are (to be supplied). In the reading of
                                the speech sounds in the regular alphabetical order
                                    (<foreign>mātṛkā</foreign>), the ten sounds that are in the
                                beginning are designated as monophthongs
                                (<foreign>samāna</foreign>)— <foreign>a ā i ī u ū ṛ ṝ ḷ ḹ</foreign>.
                                The application is in rules like <foreign>samānas savarṇe
                                    dīrghībhavati paraś ca lopam (1.2.1)</foreign>, “A monophthong
                                followed by a homogeneous sound becomes long and the latter gets
                                deleted”. [E.g.] <foreign>śivāptiḥ</foreign>, “attaining Śiva”.
                            </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <l style="sūtra" xml:id="KS-1.1.4">
                        <label>KS 1.1.4</label><anchor n="KS-1.1.4-t"/><hi>teṣāṃ dvau dvāv
                            anyonyasya savarṇau ||1.1.4||</hi>
                    </l>

                    <ab corresp="#KS-1.1.4" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#KS-1.1.4-t">Among them, within each pair, both are
                                homogeneous (<foreign>savarṇa</foreign>) with each other.</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.4-1"><label>BB 1.1.4</label><anchor n="BB-1.1.4-1-t"
                            /><hi>teṣāṃ</hi> daśānāṃ samānānāṃ madhyād yau <hi>dvau dvau</hi>
                        varṇau, tā<hi>v anyonyasya</hi> parasparaṃ <hi>savarṇa</hi>saṃjñau bhavataḥ
                        | akārasya ākāraḥ savarṇaḥ | ākārasya akāraḥ savarṇaḥ | evaṃ sarvatra |
                        pūrvam eva prayojanam |</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.4-1" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.4-1-t">Among the ten monophthongs, within each
                                pair, the two sounds are termed homogeneous
                                    <foreign>(savarṇa)</foreign> with each other. The homogeneous
                                sound of <foreign>a</foreign> is <foreign>ā</foreign>. The
                                homogeneous sound of <foreign>ā</foreign> is <foreign>a</foreign>.
                                Thus it happens everywhere. The application is just as in the
                                previous <foreign>sūtra</foreign>.</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.4-2">
                        <anchor n="BB-1.1.4-2-t"/>samānau varṇau savarṇāv ity anvarthasaṃjñāvijñānād
                        dvayor hrasvayor dvayoś ca dīrghayor anyonyaṃ sāvarṇyam | tena mamātra
                        dṛḍhāsthetyādau dīrghaparalopau bhavataḥ | </p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.4-2" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.4-2-t">By understanding it
                                    (<foreign>savarṇa</foreign>) to be a self-explanatory term that
                                denotes two similar sounds as homogeneous, homogeneity occurs
                                between two short or two long vowels as well. Therefore, in cases
                                such as <foreign>mamātra (mama + atra)</foreign>, “my here”, or
                                    <foreign>dṛḍhāsthā (dṛḍhā + āsthā)</foreign>, “firm regard”,
                                lengthening and deletion of the latter vowel take place. </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>


                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.4-3"><anchor n="BB-1.1.4-3-t"/>nanv eka evāsau varṇo dvir
                        uccāryate | naitau dvau na savarṇau | sati hi bhede dvitvaṃ samānavarṇatvaṃ
                        ca bhavati | yad āhuḥ, yatra kiñcit sāmānyaṃ kaścic ca viśeṣaḥ, sa viṣayaḥ
                        sadṛśatāyā iti | tat katham atra dvayoḥ savarṇatvam |</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.4-3" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.4-3-t">No doubt, it is only one sound uttered
                                twice. These are not two; nor are they homogeneous because duality
                                and similarity between sounds occur when there is a difference. As
                                it is said, similarity exists within the domain where there is a
                                certain degree of commonality and a certain degree of specificity.
                                Therefore, how come homogeneity exists for those two in this case?
                            </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.4-4"><anchor n="BB-1.1.4-4-t"/>ucyate, yatraiko 'kāra udāttaḥ
                        svarito vā aparas tv anudāttaḥ, tatra svarabhedāt sati dvaidhe tāvad eṣa na
                        doṣaḥ | yatrāpy eko 'kāras sānunāsiko dvitīyo niranunāsikas tatrāpi bhedād
                        eva na doṣaḥ | evam ikārādiṣu jñeyam | tathā coktam |
                        <lg type="quote">
                            <l> hrasvadīrghaplutatvāc ca traisvaryopanayena ca | </l>
                            <l> ānunāsikyabhedāc cāpy akāro 'ṣṭādaśātmakaḥ ||</l>
                        </lg>
                         iti | yatra tv abhedas tatrāpi kvacid bhedāropeṇa dvitvaṃ sadṛśatvaṃ ca
                        dṛśyate | yathā yuddhe 'rjuno 'rjuna iva prathitapratāpa ityādau | tadvad
                        ihāpi bhaviṣyati | atha vā, uccārakālādikṛtaḥ sūkṣmo viśeṣo 'vaśyambhāvī |
                        tam āśritya dvayos savarṇatvaṃ bhaviṣyati |</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.4-4" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.4-4-t">It is said: There is no problem when one
                                    <foreign>a</foreign> is acute or circumflex, and the other is
                                grave because there exists a difference due to the variation in
                                pitch accent. Even when one <foreign>a</foreign> is nasalized and
                                the other is non-nasalized, due to the very difference, there is no
                                problem either. Thus it should be understood as <foreign>i</foreign>
                                and so on. So it is stated: <l>Because of short, long, and prolonged
                                    variations and the application of three pitch accents, as well
                                    as the contrast in terms of nasality, the sound
                                        <foreign>a</foreign> is of eighteen kinds.</l> However, even
                                in cases where there is no difference, in some instances, due to the
                                imputation of difference, duality and similarity are observed. Just
                                as, “in the battle, Arjuna has renowned glory like Arjuna”,
                                similarly it will happen here as well. Or else, due to factors like
                                pronunciation timing and so on, a subtle distinction is bound to
                                occur. Depending on that (the distinction), there will be
                                homogeneity of the two. </item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>

                    <p xml:id="BB-1.1.4-5" style="stream1"><anchor n="BB-1.1.4-5-t"/>ata eva
                        vyañjanānām api samānasthānānāṃ śvāsanādaghoṣādibhede 'pi sāvarṇyam | tena
                        vaktā bhaktetyādāv asavarṇe dhuṭi cavargasya kir ucyamānaḥ pṛcchati
                        ujjhatītyādau savarṇe nāsti | evam antassthānām apy ānunāsikyabhinnānāṃ
                        savarṇatvam | tenānusvārasyāntassthāyām antaratamas sānunāsika eva
                        parasavarṇo vakṣyate | saym̐yantā | savm̐vatsaraḥ | talm̐lokam |</p>

                    <ab corresp="#BB-1.1.4-5" type="apparatus">
                        <list type="translation">
                            <item corresp="#BB-1.1.4-5-t">Consequently, there is homogeneity of
                                consonants that have the same place of articulation even though
                                there can be differences in terms of breathing
                                    (<foreign>śvāsa</foreign>), vibrating (<foreign>nāda</foreign>),
                                voicing (<foreign>ghoṣa</foreign>), etc. Therefore, a
                                    <foreign>k</foreign>-set (velar stops) replacement of the
                                    <foreign>c</foreign>-set (palatal stops/affricates) sounds
                                preceding a non-homogenous obstruent (<foreign>dhuṭ</foreign>)—as it
                                happens in words like <foreign>vaktā</foreign>, “speaker”, or
                                    <foreign>bhaktā</foreign>, “worshipper”,—does not occur when a
                                homogeneous obstruent follows as in words like
                                    <foreign>pṛcchati</foreign> “asks” or <foreign>ujjhati</foreign>
                                “abandons”. The same way, homogeneity also occurs for the
                                approximants, which are distinguished in terms of nasalisation. By
                                that (homogeneity of the approximants), it will be explained that
                                when followed by an approximant, an <foreign>anusvāra</foreign> is
                                replaced by the closest nasalized realization (of the approximant)
                                itself, which is homogeneous with the following sound (the
                                approximant). [E.g.] <foreign>saỹyantā</foreign> “controller”,
                                    <foreign>saṽvatsaraḥ</foreign> “a year”,
                                    <foreign>tal̃lokam</foreign> “their world”.</item>
                        </list>
                    </ab>
